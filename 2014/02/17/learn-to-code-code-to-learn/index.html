Learn to Code or Code to Learn?

Posted on 17 Feb 2014 by Luisa Beck

Tweet

Internalizing this switcheroo is maybe one of the single most important lessons for a student. At first glance, the words “code” and “learn” are merely shuffled around a bit. And you’re right of course. But for most people who have 12-20 years of traditional schooling behind them, who live in a culture where everything from grades to careers to computers and car engines is about performance, it’s not a simple switch.

At DBC, we try hard to fight the frustrations that students experience when their code doesn’t immediately “perform” according to their expectations. A student might have spent hours assuming their code works one way, only to discover that it breaks every time they try to make a minor adjustment. And it’s that precise moment, when a student may be so frustrated that they’re ready to punch the screen, that the “Code to learn” mantra is crucial.

Our teachers try to sit down with the student and probe their mental model of the problem. And instead of reprimanding them for not “performing”, turn their frustration into a sense of discovery and joy when they realize that their mental model of the problem is wrong.

Yes, discovery and joy. Because that realization is what learning is. It’s getting closer to really understanding the problem at hand. And correcting a student’s mental model of it.

Learning how to Learn

One of our biggest mission is to try to get students to unlearn mental habits and attitudes that prevent them from learning and growing.

Part of that is also peeling away at assumptions students have about themselves as learners. What does it mean to be “smart”? Is that even the right question to ask in the first place?

Carol Dweck’s theory of the fixed vs. growth mindset underlies a lot of this probing. Dweck, a Stanford psychologist, makes a radical claim: that cultivating a passion for learning, rather than a hunger for approval, can profoundly affect our success, motivation and most importantly, our capacity for happiness. She differentiates between what she calls a “fixed mindset” and a “growth mindset”.

The first is reflected by statements like “I’m not smart enough for this”, or “Wow, you’re so intelligent for solving this so quickly”. These statements assume that our intelligence is tied to our performance and that it’s static. Success thereby becomes an affirmation that we’re smart, so we strive to avoid failure at all costs and put ourselves into situations that affirm our “ability” (i.e. we avoid hard problems and challenges, even if we might learn from them).

Contrast this to a “growth mindset”, which assumes that we can stretch ourselves and learn new things with attention and deliberate practice. A person who cultivates a growth mindset sees challenges as potential for growth and failure as an opportunity to learn.

Aside from presenting her research, we try to create an environment where students don’t feel ashamed or judged when they struggle with a problem, but rather happy to be attempting it in the first place.

It’s not always easy, especially because so many students have a lifetime of performance-driven classroom experiences behind them. And they live in a society that makes quick judgments about them based on their performance and success. But we want DBC to be an experience that has the potential to be life-changing. Not merely in terms of teaching students a new set of coding “tools”, but a new way of engaging problems and their own capacity to learn from them.
