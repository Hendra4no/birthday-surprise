From Teacher To Coder: Inexperienced Does Not Mean Inadequate

Posted on 13 Feb 2013 by Scott James

Tweet

"Inexperience does NOT mean inadequate."

-- Kim Lanning

This week we talk to Kimberly Lanning, junior developer at Thoughtworks, a consulting agency focused on advancing software’s liberating capacity for society.

Two years ago she was teaching middle school math when she realized her love for problem solving could help even more people if she were building apps. Lanning talks about changing careers, changing coasts, and what it's like to get paid to do something she's passionate about.

Who are you? Tell us about yourself.

I am currently a developer working for a consulting company called Thoughtworks. I enjoy traveling, solving puzzles, and being outdoors.

What were you doing before? What made you decide to code?

I was a middle school math teacher on the East Coast. After teaching for a few years, I noticed that my students consistently had much better phones than I did. That's when it hit me, I could help so many more students and my fellow teacher friends if I could write free apps for them to use.

How did you learn? What were the resources that were most valuable? What surprised you about learning?

First I went through "Learn Python the Hard Way" with the assistance of my brother. Then I gained the majority of my Ruby/Rails knowledge at DBC.

What surprised me about learning: How much developers Google and the fact that it's ok to look up anything.

I was also very happy and surprised to see how supportive and helpful both the Ruby community and the Women Who Code community are in SF.

What have you been doing with it?

Currently, I am on a mostly Java project so my time is spent learning Java.

What were some mistakes you made or challenges you faced?

Inexperience does NOT mean inadequate.

What were some of the biggest obstacles you found outside the actual learning?

Finding a place to live in SF.

How do you find your life different now emotionally speaking?

My life has totally changed for the better. I changed both careers and coasts. I am always challenged at work and am constantly learning. It's amazing.

What's next for you?

My company is extremely helpful when it comes to supporting your learning and passion. I'm focusing most of my time now on learning mobile development outside of my regular consulting work with a group of fellow ThoughtWorkers.

Any closing thoughts for those thinking about learning to program?

If you like to solve puzzles, then programming is for you.

I can't believe that someone is actually paying me to play on my computer all day long and solve/find issues. I love my life and I hope that others can find an area in programming that they can be passionate about also.

Through this Pathways to Programming interview series, we talk to formerly non-technical people about how they learned to program in a non-traditional way and what they’re doing now.
