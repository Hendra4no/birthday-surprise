5 Things We Can Learn From 180 Websites In 180 Days

Posted on 29 Jul 2013 by Staff

Tweet

At Dev Bootcamp, we have a particular philosophy of learning to code. We train people passionate about building a career as a developer how to begin the process by providing enough technical value to teams to be paid to continue learning. Our model is about super intense, cohort-based learning, and although we're continuing to refine it every day, we love the results we've seen so far.

All that said, we don't believe there is only one right way to learn to code, and at the end of the day, the mission that is bigger than just our organization is to develop the next generation of technical talent.

We recently stumbled across one of the most interesting approaches to learning to code we've seen: 180 websites in 180 days.

The project comes from Jennifer Dewalt. Jennifer's background is in art, and the inspiration for her learning to code came from a desire to create within the medium through which people are now expressing themselves and communicating who they are: information technology.

About 4 months in, she has so far created 119 mini projects. They run the gamut of languages and each is posted entirely on GitHub and accompanied by an explanatory blog post. They've ranged from the practical - a To Do list maker, published yesterday - to the passionate - such as a "paint your own Mondrian" site - published on Day 4!

In all, the project is a total inspiration - not only for people who want to code, but for anyone with a goal. Here's 5 things we can all learn from it.

1. JFDI - Whether your goal is to get fit at the gym or to learn to code, the only way to start is by starting. Jennifer's approach to learning may not be for everyone, but her instinct to just simply begin rather than creating complicated and intricate plans for doing so is a great reminder for all of us.

2. Remember to set the bar high - Creating 180 projects in 180 days with no days off is no small task. Even if all of the projects were planned in advance, simply the execution would be difficult. The fact that she is both brainstorming and executing every single day makes it all the more impressive. The thing is, being a successful programmer - a successful anything, really - is about setting the bar high and striving, as much as it is the specific domain in which one is striving.

3. Part of programming is getting it wrong - When it comes to coding, perfect can often be the enemy of the good. In the context of a team that needs to continually get more out, this can be particularly damaging. By committing to present everything on GitHub every day, Jennifer is accepting that she's going to make mistakes - and that, indeed, those mistakes might be the best way to help her learn.

4. Push, push, push - At the end of the day, programming is about pushing and getting your code into the world. Jennifer's extreme pace is a reminder to always be pushing.

5. Creativity and passion haven't lost their power to inspire us - Jennifer's post about her project has exploded across the web developer community, garnering more than 300 comments on Hacker News and inspiring many admiring blog posts like this one. No matter what discipline we come from, aspiration inspires us.
