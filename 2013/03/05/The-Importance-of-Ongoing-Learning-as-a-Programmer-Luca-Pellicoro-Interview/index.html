The Importance of Ongoing Learning as a Programmer

Posted on 05 Mar 2013 by Scott James

Tweet

"You often have to let go of your assumptions about the problem in order to solve it."

-- Luca Pellicoro

This week we talk to Luca Pellicoro, a senior software engineer at a stealth-mode startup in San Francisco and a dedicated ongoing self-educator.

Being a programmer requires perpetual learning as new technologies continuously arise. Pellicoro has lived this ongoing learning, coding in Python at Gazillion Entertainment, working as a Software Engineer in Test at Playdom and Disney Interactive, and now working at a startup in Rails. He points out that "having a solid CS foundation, whether is comes from school or not, is important because core concepts like data structures and abstraction won't change. It's nice to have a framework/paradigm to fit new knowledge into. Also, the UNIX command line has existed for decades and will remain."

We talk with Luca about his continuing journey of learning, the challenge of overcoming fear of the unknown, and dealing with negative emotions as part of the coding process.

Who are you? Tell us about yourself.

I currently do web programming and UX for a Rails based stealth mode startup on Potrero Hill. I've worked in the Bay Area for a few startups and one large corporation after graduating from school in the Southeast, even though I grew up in France.

What made you decide to code?

I dabbled around with computers as a kid, gaming and such. I seriously got into software engineering in college while studying psychology. I realized coding can induce Flow which gets addictive.

How do you continually learn new coding skills? What resources are most valuable? What surprises you about ongoing learning?

Learning something truly new requires stepping out of one's comfort zone. So it's always good to challenge yourself to do things differently or by following better standards.

In terms of practical resources:

Stackoverflow: This community platform is probably responsible for pushing programmer years ahead collectively. If you have a problem, as long as you can express it correctly, someone probably had the same issue and it was likely solved.

Code academy: Great for beginning a new language.

Local tech specific meetups: For networking, learning about hot new tools, and sometimes master programmers will come down and spill their wisdom.

Classes at hacker spaces: These allowed me to practice and dive deep into a technologies. I'm thinking the ongoing front end course at Noisebridge and the D3 one at Gafta.

Another great one is reading the source code of you favorite tool or language. And with Github, it's even better to look at the earlier versions of the project, when things were simple. So you can get a sense of what the authors were after solving from the beginning.

Of course my peers, colleagues and mentors have been instrumental.

The biggest surprise came when I realized the major obstacle was the fear of the unknown.

For example, I was fortunate that my employer encouraged me to learn Rails the right way. I sat down for the 3 full days and followed a tutorial and now it's part of my tool belt.

What have you been doing with it?

Outside of work, some small pet projects to learn new technologies.

What were some mistakes you make or challenges you face?

Believing that something is not learnable.

What were some of the biggest obstacles you find outside the actual learning?

Dealing with frustration and the lack of gumption. Programming requires a certain mental stability to be able to concentrate for long periods of time but at the same time being overly obsessed and disregarding other priorities is a good trait to have. Basically time and energy are the limiting factors.

How do you find your life different now emotionally speaking as you become a more experienced coder?

The biggest obstacle is how to deal with negative emotions. The problem is usually inside you.

You often have to let go of your assumptions about the problem in order to solve it. Naps, meditation sessions, walks or conversations with co-workers always helps

Also it always feels good to be in-demand professionally.

Learning, growing. Mastering the art of programming.

Any closing thoughts for those thinking about learning to program?

Do it! Imagine having the ability to have something that does not exist in your head, then with these skills, it becomes real to you and others. Magic!
