First Annual DBC Holiday Hackathon

Posted on 20 Dec 2013 by Chapman Snowden

Tweet

Bruno Sanches a recent DBC grad, usually flies a little under the radar - he’s pretty quiet, but undoubtedly the type of guy who responds with positive enthusiasm whenever asked to do something.

Knowing that, we all shouldn’t have been surprised that he built aMazeng last week in 24 hours. While he was presenting it, the collective 100+ years of programming in the room went, “Whoa…”

The screenshot doesn’t do the project justice. Bruno created a regenerative maze (you get a unique maze every time) using a depth first search algorithm. The program defines the maze’s space with no walls, then divides the space with randomly positioned walls, each containing a randomly placed opening. The algorithm recursively calls itself until all the spaces created by the walls reach a minimum size. Oh, and the real-time positioning (and ultimate winner) is created with Node.js using socket.io.

That’s why we love Dev Bootcamp. We have those moments with so many Boots like Bruno - that moment when you just stand in amazement, marveling at how a Boots has so cleverly masked a program’s complexity in utter simplicity. It’s those moments that led us to hold our First Annual Alumni Holiday Hackathon.

Over the past year, we’ve had roughly 350 Boots attend DBC in San Francisco, bringing that number up to 450 over our 2 year history. Too often our Boots only get to program with the cohorts immediately before or after their own. So we asked, what would happen if we brought together alumni from across all cohorts together for a weekend of hacking?

To put it simply, it was beautiful. Our alumni came together over 24 hours, formed 9 teams, and showed us exactly what a roomful of current and former world class beginners are capable of (when given some computers, food and hours of Christmas music.) One team, Schemap, wrote a Ruby gem that uses the D3 library to visualize your database schema.

Another group, Middle, created a simple yet powerful solution to that inevitable question of “Where should we meet?” by suggesting locations equidistant from two parties.

We had a group, FaceOFF, bravely tackle the three.js library and, after creating their own physics library, create a 3D Javascript game.

While the alumni and judges were impressed with all the projects, what’s more impressive is what those projects represent. Remember that 100 years of programming I referenced a bit back? Well all of the alumni in the room represented a quarter of that total.

We’re all still incredibly new to this, and yet how far we’ve come over our respective journeys is nothing short of remarkable. We can’t wait to see what will come from our next Alumni Hackathon, especially as we add Chicago and New York alumni to the mix.
